John Marcus Aziz

Entrepeneur • Inventor • Architect

Resume

The Early Years

Once there was a little boy. This little boy did not have the best upbringing. His father was mentally and physically demanding, his mother was complacent and pacifying. Not entirely unheard of or unusual in today's society.

The boy was rather odd. He was smart, but overly sensitive. He hated loud noises and crowds. He hated looking people in their eyes. Instead of faces, he only recognized people by color. When he did talk, he couldn't stop talking. Then he would go days without saying a word.

He was prone to anger. He didn't sleep. Sometimes, he couldn't keep up with the other kids. He would ruminate on past events in an endless loop. He had no basic, executive functioning skills and had trouble taking care of himself. He complained about feeling trapped in his own body, but mostly in his own mind.

It would only be decades later that he was told that he was neurodivergent; a "low-need, highly functioning person on the autism spectrum." He didn't really stand a chance, as this condition will hamper him throughout this life, and come close to destroying him.

Then, around the 3rd grade, a trusted adult began sexually abusing the boy. For the next 2 years this continued, until his parents found out and put a stop to it. Not by counseling, mind you, but by moving the family to a different city.

After a couple of different stops, the now-5th grader found himself in Buffalo Grove. And he hated it. And he hated life. And he hated everyone. And he was always angry. But he never showed it. He knew that if he did, it wouldn't end well. So he acted like the others. And people thought he was happy.

The Start

And 6th grade came, and he hated it even more. He hated life, even more. And he hated everyone, even more. And he started to think for the first time about hurting himself. But he told anyone and he never showed it. And people thought he was happy.

And then 6th grade ended, and they had a stupid get-together for all the different elementary schools. But the boy did meet some nice kids. In particular, he meets a special, little girl. He can look at her face. Her color is deep yellow, perhaps sunburst orange. It was a good color. He tended to like those colors. They made him feel warm.

He doesn't know it at the time, but this special, little girl will change his life.

As the little boy goes through 7th grade, they have a lot of the same classes. The little boy and the little girl become friends. He learns that she is so young! She is brilliant! She is witty! She makes him laugh. She makes him feel good about himself.

And life doesn't seem so sad. And everyone doesn't seem so bad. And even though the bad feelings never go away, the little boy slowly thinks about hurting himself less and less. The interminable storm in his brain begins to weaken slightly.

And throughout 8th grade, he starts to notice that his feelings are changing. He thinks he likes the little girl, but she's so young. She's innocent. He's a monster. He's confused, so he doesn't say anything.

High School

The little boy and the little girl soon enter high school. It's the typical Americana upbringing. He plays football and basketball, she a cheerleader. They run cross-country together. Track and field. She plays violin. They have class together. They go to a couple of dances together.

But the little boy still has his demons. And he tries to learn to fight them without leaning on her.

And when things get so dark, so bleak, so hopeless; he tries to find solace in his church. But it doesn't work. He tries to find peace in flora and fauna. But it doesn't work. He drowns himself in seclusion. But it doesn't work. He doubles down. None of it works.

But the little boy still has the little girl. Almost everyday. She's his light in the dark. His guide. His anchor. He adores her. By now he knows he loves her. He has lived a 1000 lifetimes with her, disappointed with none of them. His only reason for living is to experience more lifetimes with her.

But he's couldn't let her know that. It would be weird. It would be too risky. Not worth the value of losing her friendship. She was not just in his circle of friends, in some ways, she was the friend. The one person around whom he felt the most free, the safest. She's still so young, though. She has no idea the weight that she's bearing.

So the little boy continues on. And he starts to get interest from some girls of his "own kind." But it's always their interest in him. He doesn't really like any of them, but he learns to love some of them just the same. But they all don't feel right. They are not her.

And they don't even really like him. He's nice enough but he's moody. Too intense. He doesn't really like to do boyfriend/girlfriend things. He's weird. Some berate him. Some cheat on him. Some just give up. None of the them work out. A running theme he would discover.

Interlude #1

On my mom's side, I am the first grandchild. Although my grandmother and I had an obvious language barrier, we had no issues communicating. I loved her and she made it no secret that I was her favorite grandchild.

My grandmother came to the States in '91-92 for a few months' visit. I told her everything. I told her about you.

One day, a couple of us decided to study at my house for some math test, possibly pre-calc. It could have been you, Goldstein, and Phil (maybe Carl). I was so excited to have my grandmother physically see you for the first time. My grandmother was excited to see the person who her grandson just couldn't stop talking about. This may have been the only time you had ever come to my house.

So we were studying in the kitchen and, at some point, I noticed my grandmother spying on you from around the corner using one of our mirrors in the living room; studying you intently, trying to get every angle.

After you left, she called me into her bedroom. She told me you were absolutely stunning, "gameela." She started referring to you as "Zini" or sometimes, "Cini". My mother could never understand what the hell she was talking about, but I knew.

And while she was around, she would encourage me in Arabic. She didn't call you a girl, she called you a woman.

"No woman wants a man who can't take care of her. Do you think Cini would? Take care of yourself or you can't take care of her." "No woman wants a man who can't cook for her. Do you think Zini would? Learn how to cook." "No woman wants a man who is stupid. Do you think Cini studies hard so that she can be with a dumb man? Study hard." "No woman wants a man that she can't introduce to her parents. You have to be presentable for Zini." "Are you working hard enough on yourself? Cini won't love you until you love yourself."

And when she left and went to Libya, I couldn't talk to her as often. Perhaps once a year, if I was lucky. She kept wanting updates about "Cini."

Towards the end of her life a few years later, she came back to the States for dialysis treatment. She stayed with us for a whole year. I cared for her the entire time. We could communicate a little better by then, but the language barrier was still an issue.

I was dating a few girls, here and there. It was more of the same. They would approach me, I would date them, and then it would it end. During that year, my grandmother... hated... every... single... one... of.. them... to their core.

And she kept bringing up Cini. What about Cini? Why aren't you talking to her? Why don't you just tell her?

And I never gave her an answer. Not even a good answer. Usually just the silence associated with not speaking Arabic well, a long sigh, or the inevitable surge of tears and I would hug her. I'd say "I love you". And she would sing the same back to me in English, "Iloveyoooou" as if it were a single word.

The Demon

And the little girl starts growing up. She's maturing. She's not a little girl anymore.

And then one day, another demon showed up at the little boy's door. This wasn't unusual by itself, but he wasn't like the others though. The others were always bringing up the past. Replaying events over and over. The mistakes. The deficiencies.

This one kept talking about tomorrow.

And the little boy didn't understand at first. Tomorrow was a long ways away. He was too consumed with surviving today. What was so special about tomorrow anyhow? In the back of his mind, he knew there was a chance that tomorrow wasn't coming anyways.

And then the little boy hears the whispers. The challengers. The ones learning. She's beautiful. She's cute. She's smart. She's fit. She's funny. She hangs with the boys. This girl is unique.

And the little boy understood the demon. He was in trouble, big trouble. And he wasn't a little boy anymore.

High School - Part 2

But the boy still had the girl. They were still friends. She was still there every day. And he loved her even more. He wants to figure her out. The way she ticks. He's obsessed with her. He wants more. He is motivated.

So the boy decides to double-down. He begins to take all the smart people classes to keep up with the girl. He needs to see her at least a little while longer. He has no other plan. This is the only way. He starts taking classes at the local community college to beef up his college application. He wants to go to school near her, with her. He tells no one.

But it's not working. He does well on his college entrance exam. He does well with his community college credits. He is doing well enough on the outside, but on the inside, his brain begins to short-circuit. His father is cutting him off. He has no money. He has to figure out how to survive on his own.

He gets into Columbia, he doesn't say anything. He gets into Brown, he doesn't say anything. Who's he going to tell? Why would he go? He can't afford it. He knows he won't finish anyways. Illinois is the only low-risk option. If something happens, then it cost him nothing. But he knows what this means. He's still in trouble.

Now all the demons appear to be coming out to play. He sees the ending, and it's not good.

So the little boy begins to lose hope. And he stops going to school. Every day a painful reminder, not only of who he was losing, but of a reality he doesn't want to return to. And the school threatens to withhold his diploma, so he relents. But he knows its over.

And graduation comes. He takes a picture with her. It will be his only tangible reminder of her.

College - Part 1

The boy goes to Illinois. The girl, to Dartmouth. The boy and the girl would talk on the rarest of occasions, but it wasn't enough for him. And he hated it. And he hated life. And he hated everyone. And he was always angry.

And he began to hurt himself. He discovered drugs. The drugs silenced the demons. But it was all fake, and he knew it was temporary. That one day, the piper would need to get paid.

One day, the boy sees the girl at a summer party. He's messed up. He's embarrassed to see her. He wants to tell her. He wants to convince her he's not like this. To hug her. To hold her. To say "I love you." Hoping she would sing the same back to me, "Iloveyoooou" as if it were a single word.

But he's too inebriated, too medicated to say anything.

The next semester, the boy would drop out of school. The brain was short-circuiting. And he hated it. And he hated life. And he hated everyone. And he was always angry.

College - Part 2

A year later, the boy goes back to school. He still hates it. He hates life. And he hates everyone. And he is still always angry. But he has to.

In the summer, he stays at school. It will be the start of his junior year. He is not looking forward to it. He already has been expelled twice. He only needs to finish out 2 semesters and he can graduate, but he just doesn't have it in him. He is tired. He is aimless. He is considering just dropping out, the ultimate plan of no-plan.

He sits in his apartment off-campus when he receives a call. It's the girl. She's in town. She wants to see him.

He's confused, but excited. He's strangely hopeful. Why would she see him? Was their friendship really that strong? Was he providing something to her, as she was to him? For the first time, the word "tomorrow" became tangible. It took on meaning. Significance.

He has little money. His clothes, embarrassing. But he is determined. Even if the girl ends their friendship, he wants to see her one more time. To get his last fix.

And the boy goes and he sees her. And she is everything he remembers and more. A woman. And he falls in love with her all over again. And a certain clarity envelopes him. And the hope returns. And he lives a 1000 lifetimes with her again.

The boy drove home and a million battles were fought over that eternity.

Was he good enough for her? No, he knew he wasn't yet. But he could be. He would graduate school. He would get his act together. He would tell her. If he wasn't the one for her, he would let her go. But this time, he would try. This was angry, but different. It was fight. It was spark. Why not him? Why not now?

And for the first time, the boy felt like he could live long enough to be a man.

But unfortunately for the boy, that semester he got a stalker. And the stalker derailed his life. And the stalker destroyed him and his plans. And the boy dropped out of school a week before a graduation and moved to St. Louis to get control of his life.

He was confused. He was defeated. And he keeps thinking of the little girl. He wants to run to her. To tell her that he tried so hard, but the universe is conspiring against him.

But he couldn't. The only thing left was to hasten his end through any/all means of self-destruction.

And that's what he did.

The Early Adult Years

After a half-decade of self-destruction; the boy has a moment of enlightenment. He has let the demons win for far too long. He is tired. He revolts.

He loves computers. They had helped him endure the long bouts of boredom by providing distraction/knowledge. He starts a business. He makes money. He opens another business. He begins to make a bit more money. The demons are retreating.

He still often thinks about the little girl. She has permanent residence in his heart. He still loves her. He wants to see her again. He wants to try again. But this time he wants it to be different. He wants to give. He's done with taking from her. He has been selfish. He has been self-centered.

And so he looks for her. And it is hard. And he doesn't find her.

Then one day, he receives a message from a classmate. There is a reunion. The boy asks about the girl. She is married and living in the west. The response crushes him. He retreats, the demons return, but he has no fight left. The boy, again, loses his spark.

The Middle Adult Years

The boy recovers. The passage of time heals some of his wounds.

He is a man now. He is married. Has children. They provide him with safety and comfort; his anchors. He still has his episodes from time to time, but they are now managed with medication.

His wife knows about the girl. She loves her. She wants to meet her. To thank her. She encourages him to try and reach out. However, he is still ashamed. Embarrassed.

In his forties, he undergoes a radical change. He begins to control all facets of his mind. He feels smarter than he ever has. More confident than he ever has. He plays with the demons now. He forces them to do his bidding and uses them to make the world a better place.

He learns AI. He learns quantum field theory. He is awarded numerous patents. He is inventor. He is a mathematician. A physicist. He talks with Nobel Prize winners. He hates wealth so he open-sources his knowledge. Dozens of companies and researchers reference his work. He plays with singularities and infinities for fun. He is not happy, but he's content, or he knows what contentment is supposed to be so he doesn't complain.

He's not a good person, but he's trying. He is fulfilled.

But he is not complete.

Because he wouldn't be where he was without that little girl. He simply wouldn't... be.

Friendship, For the Record

So he resolves to find the girl. And he does. She is a doctor!

He calls her office with only one mission. He tells her receptionist, "This is a little boy who is calling a little girl. She doesn't need to call back, but please let her know I was thinking about her and hoped she was doing well." He just wants her to know that someone is thinking about her many years later. He doesn't want anything back. It's the least that he can do.

But she did call back. And when the boy hears her voice on his voicemail, the part of his heart that had been stagnant for such a long time, suddenly springs up and sings. And he was happy and he was sad and he was scared.

He was going to call her back. He was going to tell her everything. She saved him. He owed her. She would get back what she put into the relationship. He would return it all. He wanted to be there for her. He knew that it was too late for some things, but not all. There was still a lot of life left to live.

And he called her back. And it was confusing. And they texted each other. And it was confusing. And the boy realized that perhaps everything wasn't as he thought.

The little girl didn't want to talk to him. And it killed him. Did he hurt her? Did he upset her? Was there too much time in between? Perhaps they had become strangers.

And so he wrote her. A crude letter, rough and unrefined. He sent it anyway. She did not respond.

His wife sat him down. You don't understand, she said.

Perhaps she didn't know, your letter scared her off. Perhaps she didn't care, your letter annoyed her. Perhaps she knew and you hurt her. If and when she's ready, she will reach back out.

However, she added...

"You didn't tell her that she saved your life. She will want to know. You can to tell her that you cared for her. You can tell her that you want her friendship again. She might not know, she might not care. But then once you tell her, you have to let her be."

And she was right. And it kills me.

I will love you for the rest of eternity, Cini.
